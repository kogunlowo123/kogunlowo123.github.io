Privacy Engineering Should Be a Dedicated Role

Privacy is not a subset of security. Security protects data from unauthorized access. Privacy ensures data is collected, used, and shared appropriately — even by authorized users. You need engineers who can implement consent flows, data minimization, and right-to-deletion at the code level.

The Real Trade-offs

  • Privacy is a design discipline: Privacy by design means making architectural decisions about data minimization, purpose limitation, and retention at the system level — not bolting on consent banners after launch.
  • Security engineers don't think about purpose: A security engineer ensures data is encrypted and access-controlled. A privacy engineer asks "should we be collecting this data at all?" and "does the user know how we're using it?"
  • Right-to-deletion is an engineering challenge: GDPR's right to erasure requires finding and deleting a person's data across databases, backups, logs, analytics, and third-party integrations. This needs dedicated engineering, not a compliance checkbox.
  • The talent pool is tiny: Privacy engineering requires understanding both software architecture and privacy law. Finding engineers who can implement differential privacy, k-anonymity, or federated learning is extremely difficult.
  • Counter-argument — privacy is everyone's job: Making privacy a dedicated role lets other engineers ignore it. Privacy should be embedded in engineering culture, not siloed into a specialized team.
