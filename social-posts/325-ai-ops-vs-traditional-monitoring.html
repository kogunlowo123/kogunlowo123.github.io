Your team runs Nagios, Prometheus, and Grafana — battle-tested tools that catch 95% of issues. Then management mandates an AIOps platform promising "anomaly detection" and "root cause analysis." Six months later you have two monitoring stacks, more alert noise than before, and an ML model that flags every deployment as an anomaly. AIOps is powerful, but only when it replaces — not duplicates — your existing stack.

The Real Trade-offs

  • Alert correlation is AIOps' superpower: Traditional monitoring fires 500 alerts during an outage. AIOps groups them into one incident with a probable root cause. This alone justifies the investment for teams drowning in alert fatigue.
  • Anomaly detection needs baselining time: AIOps models require 2-4 weeks of clean data to establish baselines. Deploy during a turbulent period and every normal fluctuation becomes an "anomaly."
  • Traditional monitoring wins on transparency: When Prometheus fires an alert, you know exactly why — a threshold was crossed. When Dynatrace says "anomaly detected," engineers spend time understanding why the AI flagged it.
  • Cost comparison is not even close: Prometheus + Grafana is free. Dynatrace or Datadog with AI features can cost $30-50 per host per month. At 500 hosts, that is $180K-$300K per year.
  • The hybrid approach wins: Use traditional monitoring for known failure modes with static thresholds, and AIOps for unknown-unknowns and cross-service correlation. Neither alone is sufficient.
