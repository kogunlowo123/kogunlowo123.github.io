Default-Deny Network Policies Should Be Mandatory

By default, every pod in Kubernetes can talk to every other pod. That means a compromised container can reach your database, your secrets store, and your control plane. Default-deny is not optional — it's negligent to skip.

The Real Trade-offs

  • Default-deny breaks dev environments: Developers deploying new services need to write NetworkPolicies before anything works. That adds friction to every deployment and slows iteration.
  • Egress deny is the hardest part: You need to allow DNS, external APIs, cloud metadata endpoints, and more. One missing egress rule and your app silently fails with timeout errors.
  • Not all CNIs support it: The default kubenet CNI in some managed K8s services doesn't enforce NetworkPolicies. You need Calico, Cilium, or another CNI that actually implements the spec.
  • Lateral movement prevention is worth the pain: The 2021 Codecov breach showed how a compromised CI container accessed secrets across services. Default-deny would have contained the blast radius.
  • Service mesh provides an alternative: Istio and Linkerd offer mTLS-based authorization policies that can replace NetworkPolicies with identity-based controls. But now you're running a service mesh.
