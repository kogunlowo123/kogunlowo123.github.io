Reserved Instances vs Savings Plans: Which Commitment Model Saves More?

Standard RIs offer up to 72% savings but lock you to a specific instance type in a specific region. Compute Savings Plans give up to 66% savings and apply across instance families, regions, and even Fargate/Lambda. That 6% gap is the price of flexibility — and for most organizations, flexibility wins.

The Real Trade-offs

  • Savings Plans are the default for new purchases: Unless you have a very specific reason, Compute Savings Plans should be your first commitment. They apply automatically to the most expensive on-demand usage first, work across EC2, Fargate, and Lambda, and survive architecture changes.
  • Standard RIs still win for stable, predictable workloads: If you're running the same m5.xlarge database server 24/7 for 3 years, a Standard RI with all-upfront payment saves 6% more than the equivalent Savings Plan. For infrastructure that truly never changes, that premium matters at scale.
  • The RI Marketplace is a safety net: Standard RIs can be resold if your needs change. Savings Plans cannot. If your organization frequently pivots architecture or acquires/divests business units, the ability to sell unused capacity has real value.
  • Convertible RIs are the worst of both worlds: They offer ~54% savings — less than Compute Savings Plans — while still requiring you to manage conversions manually. There's almost no scenario where Convertible RIs beat Compute Savings Plans on flexibility or savings.
  • Layer your commitments strategically: Cover your absolute baseline (always-on instances) with Standard RIs for maximum savings. Cover the next tier of predictable usage with Compute Savings Plans for flexibility. Leave variable workloads on-demand or Spot. This tiered approach maximizes total savings.
