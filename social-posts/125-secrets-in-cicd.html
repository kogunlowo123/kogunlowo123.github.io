Your CI/CD Pipeline Is Your Biggest Security Risk

Your CI/CD pipeline has admin access to AWS, can push to production, and runs arbitrary code from pull requests. It's the most privileged system in your organization, yet it's often the least secured. One compromised GitHub Action and your entire infrastructure is exposed.

The Real Trade-offs

  • OIDC eliminates static credentials: Stop storing AWS access keys as GitHub secrets. OIDC federation gives your workflows temporary, scoped credentials. This single change eliminates the most common CI/CD attack vector.
  • Third-party actions are supply chain risks: Every uses: third-party/action@v1 in your workflow is code you trust implicitly. Pin to commit SHAs, not tags. Use GitHub's allow-list for approved actions.
  • PR workflows from forks can exfiltrate secrets: If your workflow runs on pull_request_target and checks out the fork's code, a malicious PR can steal your secrets. This has happened to major open-source projects.
  • Pipeline permissions should be minimal: Your CI job that runs tests doesn't need production AWS access. Separate build, test, and deploy workflows with progressively increasing permissions.
  • SLSA and artifact signing are becoming table stakes: Sign your container images, verify your dependencies, and attest your build provenance. Supply chain attacks are not theoretical — they're happening now.
