Monorepo vs Polyrepo: You're Optimizing for the Wrong Thing

Google, Meta, and Microsoft use monorepos with custom tooling that cost millions to build. Your startup isn't Google. But your 300-repo polyrepo where nobody can find the authentication service isn't working either. The repo strategy is an organizational design decision disguised as a technical one.

The Real Trade-offs

  • Monorepos require custom tooling: Without Nx, Turborepo, or Bazel, a monorepo means running all tests on every commit. The tooling investment is non-trivial — Google built their own VCS for this.
  • Polyrepo creates diamond dependency hell: Service A depends on library v1.2, Service B depends on v1.3, and they both depend on each other. Coordinating version bumps across 50 repos is a full-time job.
  • Atomic cross-cutting changes are monorepo's killer feature: Renaming an API field across 20 consumers in one PR is trivial in a monorepo and a multi-week coordination effort in polyrepo.
  • Polyrepo enables team autonomy: Each team owns their repo, their CI, their deploy cadence. No fighting over shared CODEOWNERS or build configurations. Independence has real velocity benefits.
  • The actual question is team coupling: Tightly coupled teams sharing interfaces need a monorepo. Independent teams with stable contracts work better with polyrepo. Match the repo to the org chart.
