The Real Trade-offs

  • ZTNA eliminates lateral movement: VPNs place users ON the network. Once in, they can move laterally. ZTNA connects users to specific applications — they never touch the network. This is fundamentally more secure.
  • Legacy apps don't fit cleanly: ZTNA works great for web apps and APIs. But thick clients, RDP, SSH, and custom TCP protocols require agent-based ZTNA or tunneling — which starts looking a lot like a VPN.
  • Vendor lock-in is the new risk: Zscaler, Cloudflare, or Palo Alto become your new perimeter. If they have an outage, nobody works. You've traded one single point of failure for another — just a different vendor.
  • Cost can exceed VPN at scale: Enterprise ZTNA platforms charge per-user-per-month. For 10,000 users at $10/user/month, that's $100K/year. A VPN concentrator costs a fraction of that. The security improvement must justify the premium.
  • The transition takes years, not months: You can't rip out VPN overnight. Most organizations run VPN and ZTNA in parallel for 1-2 years. The operational overhead of running both is often underestimated.
