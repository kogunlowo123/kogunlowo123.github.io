Snowflake vs Databricks — The $100B Platform War

Snowflake started as a SQL warehouse and bolted on Snowpark for Python/ML. Databricks started as a Spark notebook platform and bolted on SQL warehousing. They're converging from opposite directions, and both want to be your single data platform. The question isn't which is better — it's which direction of convergence has fewer gaps for your team.

The Real Trade-offs

  • SQL analysts love Snowflake: The SQL experience is best-in-class — virtual warehouses, time travel, zero-copy clones, and a governance model that makes sense to data teams. If your primary users write SQL, Snowflake's ergonomics win.
  • Data scientists love Databricks: Notebooks, MLflow, Spark DataFrames, GPU clusters — Databricks is where ML teams feel at home. The notebook-first workflow with integrated experiment tracking is genuinely productive for iterative model development.
  • Open formats vs. proprietary storage: Databricks bet on Delta Lake (now open-source via Linux Foundation). Your data sits in your cloud storage in Parquet. Snowflake stores data in a proprietary format — great for performance, harder to escape. This matters for long-term flexibility.
  • Cost comparison is workload-dependent: Snowflake's credit-based pricing can spike with concurrent queries. Databricks' DBU pricing varies by instance type. For SQL-heavy analytics, Snowflake is often cheaper. For ML training and Spark workloads, Databricks usually wins.
  • Many enterprises run both: Snowflake for the analytics/BI layer, Databricks for ML and data engineering. This isn't ideal but reflects reality — neither platform fully replaces the other yet. Unity Catalog and Snowflake's governance are converging but not equivalent.
