Savings Plans Made Reserved Instances Obsolete

Savings Plans commit to a $/hr spend — not a specific instance type, family, or even service. Switch from EC2 to Fargate? Still covered. Move from m5 to m7g? Still covered. Why is anyone still buying RIs?

The Real Trade-offs

  • Compute Savings Plans are the sweet spot: Commit $/hr and apply to any EC2 instance, Fargate task, or Lambda invocation across any region. This flexibility is worth the slightly lower discount vs EC2-specific plans.
  • RIs still have one edge — RDS and ElastiCache: Savings Plans don't cover RDS, ElastiCache, Redshift, or OpenSearch. For these services, Reserved Instances are still the only commitment discount option.
  • Stacking SPs and RIs is complex: AWS applies RIs first, then Savings Plans. Most FinOps teams struggle with the interaction between the two. Simplifying to Savings Plans only reduces cognitive overhead.
  • No marketplace for Savings Plans: Unlike Standard RIs, you can't sell unused Savings Plans. If your usage drops, you're stuck paying. This makes over-commitment riskier with SPs than with sellable RIs.
  • Recommendation tools are better for SPs: AWS Cost Explorer's Savings Plans recommendations are more accurate and actionable than RI recommendations. The simpler model makes automated purchasing feasible.
