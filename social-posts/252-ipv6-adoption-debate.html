IPv6 in the Cloud Is Still Optional — and That's Fine

The IPv6 evangelists have been saying "this is the year" for two decades. Meanwhile, 99% of cloud workloads run perfectly fine on IPv4. Dual-stack adds complexity, breaks security tooling, and solves a problem most teams don't have.

The Real Trade-offs

  • IPv4 exhaustion is a provider problem, not yours: Cloud providers have massive IPv4 pools. Your private VPC uses RFC 1918 space — you'll never run out. Public IPv4 addresses now cost $0.005/hr on AWS, but most workloads don't need them.
  • Dual-stack doubles your security surface: Every security group needs IPv4 and IPv6 rules. Every NACL needs dual entries. Your WAF needs dual rules. Your monitoring needs to understand both protocols. That's real operational overhead.
  • Some services still don't fully support IPv6: Despite years of effort, not every AWS service works perfectly with IPv6. VPC peering across regions, certain managed services, and third-party integrations can surprise you.
  • IPv6-only VPCs are the real future: Instead of dual-stack, AWS is pushing IPv6-only VPCs with DNS64/NAT64 for legacy IPv4 endpoints. This is cleaner than dual-stack but requires service maturity.
  • The counterpoint — IoT and mobile need it: If you serve billions of mobile devices or IoT endpoints, many carrier networks are IPv6-native. You might genuinely need it, but that's a small percentage of cloud workloads.
