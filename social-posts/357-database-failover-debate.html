Automated Database Failover Causes More Outages Than It Prevents

Controversial take: automated database failover is one of the biggest sources of self-inflicted outages. False positive health checks, split-brain scenarios, and connection pool storms during promotion cause more downtime than the failures they're designed to prevent.

The Real Trade-offs

  • False positives trigger unnecessary failovers: A momentary CPU spike or network blip triggers a health check failure. Automated failover kicks in, causing 60-120 seconds of downtime for a problem that would have self-resolved in 5 seconds.
  • Connection pool storms amplify the outage: When the DNS endpoint changes, every application connection dies simultaneously. Hundreds of apps trying to reconnect at once overwhelm the new primary, creating a cascading failure.
  • Split-brain is the nightmare scenario: If the old primary comes back before all clients switch, you get two writers. Data divergence is incredibly hard to detect and even harder to repair.
  • RDS Proxy mitigates but doesn't solve: Connection pooling helps with reconnection storms, but adds latency, cost, and another failure point in the chain.
  • Counter-argument — manual failover is slower and riskier: Waiting for a human to assess and trigger failover at 3 AM means minutes of total outage. Automated failover with proper tuning is still faster than paging an on-call engineer.
