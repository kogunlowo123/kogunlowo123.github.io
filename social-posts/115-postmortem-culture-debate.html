Blameless Postmortems Are a Fantasy

Every SRE book says "blameless postmortems." In practice, everyone in the room knows who pushed the bad commit. The postmortem says "a configuration change caused the outage" but the Slack thread says "Dave broke prod again." True blamelessness requires psychological safety most companies haven't earned.

The Real Trade-offs

  • Blameless doesn't mean accountability-free: The goal isn't to pretend nobody made a mistake. It's to focus on why the system allowed the mistake to cause an outage. "Why didn't we catch this in CI?" not "Why did Dave merge without tests?"
  • Psychological safety is a prerequisite: If engineers fear being fired for outages, they'll hide information during postmortems. Blamelessness only works when the culture genuinely protects people.
  • Action items die in JIRA: Most postmortems produce 5-10 action items. Two weeks later, they're buried in the backlog. Without tracking completion, postmortems are just therapy sessions.
  • Not every incident needs a postmortem: A 5-minute blip that auto-recovered doesn't need a 2-hour review. Reserve formal postmortems for incidents that exceeded SLO impact or revealed systemic issues.
  • Learning from incidents is a discipline: The best teams make postmortem findings visible — weekly reviews, shared databases, pattern analysis. Isolated documents nobody reads are wasted effort.
