Reserved Instances Are Financial Bets, Not Savings

Your CTO bought 3-year All Upfront RIs for $2M. Six months later, you migrated to containers and half those instances are idle. The "72% savings" turned into a $1M sunk cost. RIs aren't savings — they're options contracts with no exit clause.

The Real Trade-offs

  • 3-year terms assume stability that doesn't exist: Architecture changes, migrations, acquisitions, and growth shifts make 3-year forecasts fiction. 1-year No Upfront RIs reduce risk, but also reduce savings.
  • Utilization monitoring is essential: Unused RIs are pure waste. AWS Cost Explorer shows RI utilization, but most teams don't check until the quarterly review — when it's too late to adjust.
  • The RI Marketplace is limited: You can sell Standard RIs on the marketplace, but only in certain regions, and buyers are scarce for odd configurations. It's not a liquid market.
  • Convertible RIs are the compromise: Lower savings (54% vs 72%) but you can exchange them for different instance types. This flexibility is worth the reduced discount for most evolving workloads.
  • The counterpoint — databases are stable: RDS, ElastiCache, and Redshift workloads rarely change size or type. For these stable, long-running workloads, 3-year RIs genuinely save money with minimal risk.
