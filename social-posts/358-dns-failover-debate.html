The Real Trade-offs

  • DNS caching is unpredictable: Even with a 60-second TTL, many resolvers cache for 5+ minutes. Java's InetAddress caches DNS indefinitely by default. Your failover time is controlled by systems you don't own.
  • Anycast eliminates the DNS problem: AWS Global Accelerator and GCP Global Load Balancer use anycast IPs that route at the network layer. No DNS change needed — traffic automatically reroutes to healthy endpoints.
  • Global Accelerator adds cost and complexity: At $0.025/hour per accelerator plus data processing fees, it's significantly more expensive than Route 53 failover routing. Is sub-second failover worth the premium?
  • Client-side retries mask DNS slowness: Well-designed clients with retry logic and circuit breakers can tolerate DNS propagation delays. The problem isn't DNS — it's brittle client implementations.
  • Counter-argument — DNS is battle-tested: DNS failover has worked reliably for decades. It's simple, cheap, and understood by every ops team. Replacing it with proprietary services adds vendor lock-in.
