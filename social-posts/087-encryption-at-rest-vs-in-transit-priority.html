AWS encrypts your EBS volumes by default. Congratulations — you're protected against someone physically stealing a hard drive from an AWS data center. Meanwhile, your internal services communicate over plaintext HTTP, and anyone with network access can sniff credentials in real-time. Which encryption actually protects your data?

The Real Trade-offs

  • At-rest encryption is a compliance checkbox: PCI DSS, HIPAA, and SOC 2 all require it. But cloud providers encrypt storage by default now — the threat model of physical disk theft from a data center is virtually zero. The real risk is unauthorized API access, and encryption at rest does nothing there.
  • In-transit encryption prevents real attacks: Man-in-the-middle attacks, network sniffing, and credential interception happen over unencrypted connections. Enforcing TLS 1.3 everywhere and mTLS between services stops actual attack vectors seen in the wild.
  • Client-side encryption is the real answer: Neither at-rest nor in-transit matters if the cloud provider can decrypt your data. Client-side encryption with customer-managed keys means even a compromised cloud admin can't read your data — but it breaks server-side processing entirely.
  • Performance costs differ dramatically: At-rest encryption with AES-256 is essentially free — hardware acceleration handles it. TLS handshakes add latency to every connection. mTLS between microservices with 1000 requests/second creates real CPU overhead.
  • Key management is the real problem: Both encryption types are only as strong as your key management. A KMS key policy that grants decrypt to *, or TLS certificates stored in plaintext — you have encryption everywhere and security nowhere.

If you had to fix one first — unencrypted storage or unencrypted service-to-service traffic — which would you choose?
