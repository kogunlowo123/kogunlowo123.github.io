Trunk-Based Development vs GitFlow: Your Branching Strategy Is a Culture Test

DORA research says trunk-based development correlates with elite performance. But correlation is not causation. GitFlow exists because teams ship to customers who won't accept continuous deployment. Telling a bank to "just merge to main" ignores regulatory release windows.

The Real Trade-offs

  • Trunk-based requires test maturity: Merging to main daily only works if your test suite catches regressions in minutes. Most teams don't have that. Without it, trunk-based means "break main daily."
  • GitFlow exists for a reason: Regulated industries need auditable release branches. Mobile apps ship to app stores with review cycles. GitFlow's ceremony matches these real constraints.
  • Merge conflicts reveal the real issue: Teams adopt GitFlow to avoid conflicts, but long-lived branches make conflicts worse. The conflict frequency is a symptom of unclear ownership boundaries, not branching strategy.
  • GitHub Flow is the pragmatic middle: Main + feature branches + PRs. No develop branch, no release branches. Simple enough for most teams, structured enough for code review.
  • The DORA data has selection bias: Companies mature enough to do trunk-based development are mature in other ways too — testing, monitoring, incident response. The branching model is the symptom, not the cause, of high performance.
