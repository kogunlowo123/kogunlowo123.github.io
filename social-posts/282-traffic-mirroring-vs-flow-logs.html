Traffic Mirroring vs VPC Flow Logs: Full Packet Capture vs Metadata

VPC Flow Logs tell you who talked to whom and whether it was accepted or rejected. Traffic Mirroring gives you the actual packet payload — every byte of the conversation. Flow Logs cost pennies; mirroring can cost hundreds. The question: does your security posture require seeing inside the packets?

The Real Trade-offs

  • Flow Logs are the baseline — always enable them: At $0.023/GB to S3, Flow Logs for an entire VPC costs dollars per month for most workloads. They answer "did connection X happen?" which covers 95% of troubleshooting and security investigation needs. There's no reason not to have them on.
  • Traffic Mirroring is for forensic-grade visibility: When you need to see HTTP request bodies, SQL queries, or detect data exfiltration payloads, Flow Logs aren't enough. Traffic Mirroring feeds packet data to tools like Zeek, Suricata, or Wireshark for deep analysis.
  • Cost scales with traffic volume, not instance count: Mirroring a busy web server generates gigabytes of captured data per hour. You need compute to receive it (NLB + analysis fleet), storage to retain it, and tooling to search it. Budget $500-2,000/month per monitored high-traffic instance.
  • Selective mirroring is the practical approach: Mirror only sensitive workloads — database servers, payment processing instances, or admin interfaces. Use Flow Logs everywhere else. Mirror filters can scope to specific ports or protocols to reduce volume by 80%+.
  • Encrypted traffic limits mirroring value: With TLS everywhere, mirrored packets are encrypted payloads. Without TLS termination before the mirror point, you capture ciphertext. This is why many teams mirror only east-west traffic between microservices where mTLS termination happens at the service mesh.
