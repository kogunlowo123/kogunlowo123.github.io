CQRS vs Traditional CRUD: Overengineered Hype or Essential Pattern?

CQRS advocates say "separate your reads from writes for infinite scalability." CRUD purists say "you just added two databases, a message bus, and eventual consistency bugs for a feature that gets 100 requests/minute." Who's right — and at what scale does the answer change?

The Real Trade-offs

  • CQRS with DynamoDB for writes and OpenSearch for reads lets each store optimize for its workload — but you now manage two data stores and eventual consistency.
  • Traditional CRUD with RDS read replicas scales reads to 5 replicas per primary. For most apps, that's 50,000+ read queries/second without CQRS complexity.
  • The Azure CQRS reference architecture shows the full pattern — but even Microsoft warns "don't apply CQRS to every bounded context."
  • CQRS shines when read and write models are fundamentally different — e-commerce product catalogs (denormalized reads) vs order processing (normalized writes).
  • Cosmos DB Change Feed makes the projection pipeline trivial, but change feed consumers can lag — your UI might show stale data for seconds after a write.
  • The honest answer: if you have fewer than 10,000 concurrent users and your read/write ratio is under 10:1, CQRS is overengineering. Start with CRUD, add read replicas, and refactor only when you hit real bottlenecks.
